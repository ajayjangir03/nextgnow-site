What is 5G Advanced and Why Should You Care?

Think of 5G Advanced as a software upgrade to the 5G network already around you — no new towers, dramatically smarter. 3GPP's Release 18 quietly sets the stage for everything from real-time factory robots to cars that talk to traffic lights.

You've probably heard that 5G is already here — and in most major cities, it is. But here's something the headlines don't always mention: the 5G you're using today is just version one. The real leap forward has a name: 5G Advanced.

In telecom, "5G Advanced" is the label for everything that comes out of 3GPP's Release 18 and beyond. Think of it less like a new generation and more like a major OS update — the hardware infrastructure largely stays the same, but the software, protocols, and capabilities get dramatically smarter.

Simple analogy

Imagine your smartphone from 2019. It ran fine. Then a software update arrived — suddenly it had new camera features, better battery management, and smarter multitasking. You didn't buy new hardware. The phone just got better. That's exactly what 5G Advanced does to mobile networks.

What actually changed in Release 18?

3GPP — the international body that writes the rules for mobile networks — finalised Release 18 in early 2024. This document runs to thousands of pages of technical specifications, but here are the things that matter to real people and industries.

Feature What it means Who benefits
AI/ML in the Radio Networks learn traffic patterns and self-optimise Everyone
RedCap enhancements Cheaper, longer-battery 5G chips for IoT devices Smart Cities, Industry
NTN (Satellite) Direct 5G connectivity via satellite — no dead zones Rural areas, Aviation
XR & gaming latency Sub-10ms response times for AR/VR experiences Consumers, Developers
Sidelink V2X Cars talking directly to each other and infrastructure Automotive

The satellite story is huge

One of the most underreported parts of 5G Advanced is NTN — Non-Terrestrial Networks. For the first time in 3GPP history, the specification formally defines how standard 5G devices can communicate with satellites in low Earth orbit.

This is a big deal. It means that in the future, your regular phone — not a special satellite phone — could maintain connectivity when you're in a mountain range, on a flight, or in a remote village where no tower has ever been built.

For the first time, a single mobile specification covers both ground-based and space-based connectivity. The gap between "coverage area" and "everywhere on Earth" just got a lot smaller. — 3GPP Release 18 Technical Overview, March 2024

RedCap: The unsung hero for IoT

Not every device needs blazing fast speeds. A parking sensor doesn't need 1 Gbps. A factory temperature monitor doesn't need ultra-low latency gaming performance. But today, connecting these devices to 5G requires the same expensive, power-hungry chip as a smartphone.

RedCap (Reduced Capability) changes this. It's a simplified 5G specification designed for devices that need to be small, cheap, and long-lasting on battery. Release 18 takes RedCap even further, enabling a new category called "eRedCap" for even simpler devices.

Key concept · RedCap

RedCap devices use a reduced number of antennas, a narrower bandwidth, and simpler processing to achieve lower cost and longer battery life — while still connecting to the same 5G network. Expected to power billions of IoT devices by 2028.

What comes after 5G Advanced?

5G Advanced is not the end of the road — it's the bridge. While networks are still rolling out Release 18 features, 3GPP is already drafting Release 19 and early-stage work on Release 20 has begun. And behind closed doors, the first technical papers on 6G are being written.

The ITU has already published its IMT-2030 framework — the official vision document for what 6G should achieve. Target: commercial deployments around 2030. That's less than six years away.

Key takeaways
  • 5G Advanced (Release 18) is an evolution, not a revolution — same towers, smarter software
  • Satellite connectivity means true global coverage from a standard phone, no special hardware
  • RedCap will enable billions of low-cost IoT devices on 5G networks
  • Vehicles, factories, AR/VR, and smart cities are the primary early beneficiaries
  • 6G standardisation is already underway — expect the first specs around 2028
